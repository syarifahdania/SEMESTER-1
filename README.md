## 🔌 Digital Logic · SECR1013

Digital Logic was my first encounter with computing at its most fundamental level — the point where mathematics becomes machine. The course covered Boolean algebra, logic gates, combinational and sequential circuits, multiplexers, and binary arithmetic, building toward an understanding of how physical hardware processes information.

What surprised me most was how something as complex as a computer could be traced all the way back to simple on/off switches. Working through Karnaugh maps and circuit diagrams trained me to think in layers of abstraction — a skill I now use constantly when reasoning about systems at every level of the stack. This course quietly shaped how I decompose problems, and I consider it one of the most foundational things I studied in my entire degree.

Going forward, I want to strengthen my understanding of sequential circuit timing and state machines, as these concepts surface again in embedded systems and hardware-adjacent fields that increasingly intersect with data engineering.

## 🧮 Discrete Structure · SECI1013

Discrete Structure introduced the mathematical language that underlies nearly all of computer science — sets, relations, graph theory, combinatorics, and formal logic. Unlike the mathematics I had studied before, this course dealt entirely with countable, structured systems rather than continuous functions.

The early weeks on proofs and set theory felt abstract in a way that was difficult to connect to practical work. That changed when we reached graph theory. Suddenly the formalism had a face — graphs model networks, dependencies, social connections, and routing logic. That realisation reframed the entire course for me and significantly sharpened my ability to reason about data relationships structurally rather than intuitively.

I intend to keep practising graph traversal algorithms and their matrix representations, as these appear repeatedly in network analysis, recommendation systems, and distributed data architectures.

## ⚖️ Integrity and Anti-Corruption Course · ULRS1032

This course examined the nature, mechanisms, and consequences of corruption, situating professional integrity within both legal and moral frameworks. The Malaysian anti-corruption landscape, whistleblowing protections, and ethical decision-making in institutional contexts formed the core of the curriculum.

I came in expecting a straightforward ethics module and left with a more nuanced understanding of how systemic corruption takes root — and how difficult it is to uproot once normalised. As someone entering a field where data is power, this course planted an important question I now carry into every project: who benefits from this, and who might be harmed by it? Data governance and transparency are not just technical concerns; they are ethical ones.

## 💻 Programming Technique I · SECJ1013

This course built the programming foundation I still build on today. Starting from variables and control structures and progressing through functions and arrays, it was less about learning syntax and more about developing a structured, patient approach to problem-solving.

Two things changed how I think permanently. First, debugging — the process of hunting down errors taught me more about logic and precision than writing code ever did. Second, learning to break a large problem into small, independently testable pieces became a habit I apply to everything from academic projects to real-world pipelines. This course made me a programmer; the ones that followed made me an engineer.

I want to keep refining my habit of writing clean, modular code from the outset rather than refactoring later — especially in collaborative environments where clarity is a form of respect toward teammates.

## 🌐 Technology and Information System · SECP1513

Rather than focusing on code, this course examined how information systems fit into the wider world — how they are designed to serve organisations, what makes them succeed or fail, and how technology decisions ripple through businesses and society.

Understanding the architecture of systems like ERP and decision support tools gave me the broader context I needed to make sense of why I was learning what I was learning. Technical skills do not exist in isolation — they are in service of people and processes. This course gave me the professional perspective to see my role as a data engineer not as building pipelines, but as enabling better decisions for the organisations and communities those pipelines serve.
